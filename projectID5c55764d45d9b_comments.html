Some things to note:

In this work, some words intentionally seem to use the wide Hebrew letters mentioned below. Please preserve them.

When a Hebrew phrase crosses a line-break, the text after the line-break (naturally) follows the text on the previous line, but the beginning of that phrase is of course also to the right. This constant swapping of left-to-right and right-to-left script may take some getting used to.

When footnotes contain Hebrew text, the footnote number is swapped to the right side of the page. In transcription, we of course follow phonetic order, and thus place it left.

Transcribing Hebrew

Hebrew script is used for the Hebrew language (in which the Old Testament originally was written), as well as modern Hebrew (Ivrit), used in Israel. Finally, Hebrew script is used for Yiddish, a language closely related to German.

The most conspicious differences with our alphabet are that it is written from right to left, and in principle only expresses the consonants. Some vowels are sometimes indicated with the letters א‎ or י‎. For learners, and to make sure the Torah is pronounced correctly, a system of diacritical marks was developed to indicate the vowels; however, in most running texts these are not used.

Another feature to be aware of is the use of final letters. Five letters have a slightly different form when they appear at the end of a word:

כ‎ k versus ך‎ final k,
מ‎ m versus ם‎ final m,
נ‎ n versus ן‎ final n,
פ‎ p versus ף‎ final p, and
צ‎ ts versus ץ‎ final ts.

Hebrew fragments are to be placed between [HE: … ].

Besides the common punctuation marks, you may encounter a few uniquely Hebrew punctuation marks. An important one is the geresh, which looks like a prime ׳‎, and is used to indicate a single letter is used as a numeral, as well as for indication abbreviations and representing non-Hebrew sounds. It is to be transcribed as #. The gershayim, ״‎ is the same symbol doubled, used to indicate acronyms and multi-letter numerals. Just type " to represent it. The year 5779 is written in Hebrew as ה׳תשע״ט‎ and can be transcribed as [HE: h#tS`"T] [Interpretation: ה‎ (5 × 1000) + ת‎ (400) + ש‎ (300) + ע‎ (70) + ט‎ (9) = 5779].

Since this transcription scheme was mostly designed to transcribe single words in an otherwise Latin script context, the full stop is used to represent the diacritical mark dagesh. If a full stop in a Hebrew context needs to be represented, please use {.}. Similarly, the colon and the quotation marks can be inserted using {:}, {’} and {"}. Please do not end the Hebrew context for a punctuation mark, as that will introduce swapping, due to the change of directionality.

In religious texts you may encounter cantilation marks, an elaborate system of indicating the way texts are to be sung or chanted. When they appear in a relevant text, I will update these instructions.

A few letters may be elongated. This is mostly a typographic feature, used to justify lines, and can normally be ignored (not all fonts make the distinction, so you may not see any difference in the examples below).
